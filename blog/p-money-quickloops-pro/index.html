P-Money: from student radio to global charts

Peter Wadams, known as P-Money, helped shape the sound of New Zealand hip-hop and took it to the world. His career moves from radio and DJ battles to platinum records, global collaborations, and music software built for real performance.

The early years: 95bFM and the Auckland underground

P-Money's story starts at Auckland student radio station 95bFM, where hip-hop was given space long before it was mainstream in New Zealand. He began as a young volunteer on the True School Hip-Hop Show, surrounded by selectors and hosts deeply connected to the culture.

That weekly environment was his classroom. It kept him close to new underground releases and taught him how global hip-hop sounds could be adapted to a local voice in Aotearoa.

Turntablism era: discipline, precision, and world-level results

Before he was known as a hit producer, he built his reputation in the DJ battle scene. Between 1999 and 2001, he won the ITF New Zealand title three years in a row, then won both the NZ DMC Finals and ITF national finals in 2001.

He went on to place third equal at the DMC World DJ Championships in London, becoming the first New Zealander to reach that podium. That period defined his performance mindset: technical skill matters, but crowd recognition and musical impact matter just as much.

Building a producer identity: Che-Fu, Navigator, and Big Things

Around the same time, P-Money expanded from turntables into full production work. His contributions to Che-Fu's Navigator included key scratches and production credits, helping the album bridge hip-hop authenticity with broader commercial songwriting.

In 2002, he co-founded Dirty Records and released his debut album Big Things. It featured major local talent and quickly went double platinum, establishing him not just as a DJ, but as a producer with a clear sonic vision.

The Scribe era and chart dominance

The partnership with Scribe changed New Zealand hip-hop permanently. "Stand Up" hit number one and stayed there for 12 non-consecutive weeks, while "Not Many" became one of the defining tracks of its generation.

Scribe's debut album The Crusader sold at blockbuster levels in both New Zealand and Australia. P-Money's production on that run earned major industry recognition, including Producer of the Year and Songwriter of the Year.

International expansion: Magic City to New York

P-Money's second album Magic City marked a clear global push, including collaborations with a pre-breakthrough Akon and other respected US artists. He later moved to New York, signed with Duck Down, and deepened his work with top-tier lyricists.

His fully produced album with Buckshot, BackPack Travels, charted on the US Billboard Rap Albums chart and featured names like Joey Bada$$. It was a major milestone for a producer from New Zealand operating in the American rap market.

Range and reinvention: pop crossover and soul records

P-Money has never stayed in one lane. "Everything" with Vince Harder showed a successful electronic-pop pivot and reached number one in New Zealand, while also getting UK radio momentum.

He also co-produced Aaradhna's Treble and Reverb, one of the most critically celebrated albums of its era, with a retro soul direction that won multiple major New Zealand Music Awards including Album of the Year.

The billion-stream milestone

In 2016, he co-wrote and produced "Call On Me" for Starley. The Ryan Riback remix turned it into a global phenomenon, eventually surpassing one billion combined streams across major platforms.

The song went multi-platinum in multiple countries and earned APRA AMCOS recognition on the 1,000,000,000 List, placing P-Money in rare global company as a songwriter-producer from Aotearoa.

Beyond records: A&R and software innovation

From 2017 to 2020, P-Money served as Senior A&R at Sony Music New Zealand, helping shape artist development in a major-label setting while keeping a strong connection to local culture.

He now builds tools for DJs and producers through QuickLoops Pro, a desktop looper designed for vinyl workflows with features like dual-deck looping, instant loop capture, DVS compatibility, and MIDI mapping.

Why this career matters

P-Money's path is a blueprint for modern independent creators: master the craft, build your own infrastructure, and keep evolving. His work helped professionalize New Zealand hip-hop, proved local production could compete globally, and now extends into music technology built from real-world DJ needs.
